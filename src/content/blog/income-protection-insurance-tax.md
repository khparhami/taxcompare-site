Income protection insurance replaces a portion of your income if you can't work due to illness or injury. It's one of the most important financial safety nets for working Australians — and it comes with a valuable tax deduction. But the tax treatment works in both directions: premiums are deductible, and benefits are taxable.

## What Is Income Protection Insurance?

Income protection insurance pays a regular benefit — typically **up to 70–85% of your pre-disability income** — while you're unable to work due to sickness or injury. Benefits are paid monthly for the benefit period (which can range from 2 years to age 65, depending on your policy).

It is distinct from:
- **Life insurance** — pays a lump sum on death
- **Total and permanent disability (TPD) insurance** — pays a lump sum if you can never work again
- **Trauma insurance** — pays a lump sum upon diagnosis of specified conditions

## Are Premiums Tax-Deductible?

**Yes — if you pay the premiums directly (outside of super).**

Income protection insurance premiums paid personally are fully deductible under section 8-1 of the Income Tax Assessment Act 1997. The deduction reduces your taxable income in the year the premium is paid.

**Example:** $2,400 annual premium at 30% marginal rate = **$720 tax saving** (effective after-tax cost of only $1,680).

At 37% marginal rate: $2,400 × 37% = **$888 saving** (effective cost: $1,512).

This makes income protection one of the most tax-efficient insurance products available to individuals.

## The Exception: Inside Super

If your income protection insurance is held **inside your super fund** as a group policy:

- The fund pays the premium and claims the deduction at the fund's 15% rate
- **You** cannot claim a personal deduction — the deduction belongs to the fund, not you
- You don't pay the premium from your own pocket, so there's nothing for you to claim

Many employer super funds include default income protection cover automatically. Check your annual super statement to see what cover you have and whether it's funded by the fund (inside super) or deducted from your bank account (outside super).

## Are Benefit Payments Taxable?

**Yes.** Income protection benefit payments are treated as assessable income and taxed at your marginal rate. This is the trade-off for the deductible premiums.

- Your insurer withholds tax from monthly benefit payments (similar to PAYG withholding on salary)
- You declare benefits in your tax return as income
- Medicare levy applies on benefits if you're a resident

The benefit amount is typically capped at 70–75% of your pre-disability income partly because it is taxed — your after-tax benefit is closer to your normal after-tax salary.

## Life, TPD, and Trauma Premiums — NOT Deductible

A common misconception is that all insurance premiums are deductible. Only income protection insurance premiums are deductible as they protect against loss of income. The others protect capital values:

| Insurance type | Premium deductible? | Benefit taxable? |
|---|---|---|
| Income protection | **Yes** | Yes (assessable income) |
| Life insurance | No | Generally no (not income) |
| TPD insurance | No | Depends on structure |
| Trauma insurance | No | Generally no |

Some life and TPD insurance held inside super has different tax treatment — consult a financial adviser for the specifics of your policy.

## What the ATO Looks For

The ATO expects the premium to be for a "risk only" product that protects your income-earning capacity. Premiums for policies that include an investment component (such as some bundled whole-of-life policies) are split — only the income protection portion is deductible. Your insurer's tax statement should break this down.

## Practical Checklist

1. **Check whether your income protection is inside or outside super.** Look at your super fund's annual statement — if a premium is being deducted from your super balance, it's inside super and not claimable personally.

2. **If paying outside super, get a tax invoice from your insurer.** Insurers typically send an annual premium statement. Keep it with your tax records.

3. **Claim it in the income year the premium was paid.** If you paid a lump-sum annual premium on 15 March, it's deductible in the 2025–26 return (for premiums paid March 2026).

4. **Prepaying 12 months before 30 June** can bring forward the deduction — useful for a high-income year.

5. **Declare any benefits received.** If you made a claim and received monthly payments, they go in your return as income.

## How Much Does the Deduction Actually Help?

At your marginal rate, a $200/month ($2,400/year) income protection premium has the following after-tax cost:

| Marginal rate | Annual premium | Annual tax saving | After-tax cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 16% | $2,400 | $384 | $2,016 |
| 30% | $2,400 | $720 | $1,680 |
| 37% | $2,400 | $888 | $1,512 |
| 45% | $2,400 | $1,080 | $1,320 |
