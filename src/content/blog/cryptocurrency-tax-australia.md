Despite what early adopters may have hoped, cryptocurrency is not a tax-free investment in Australia. The ATO has been clear since 2014: crypto is a capital asset subject to Capital Gains Tax (CGT), and every disposal triggers a reportable event. With the ATO receiving data from exchanges via the Tax File Number system, unreported crypto gains are increasingly detectable.

## How the ATO Treats Cryptocurrency

The ATO does **not** treat cryptocurrency as money or foreign currency. It is treated as a **capital asset** — in the same category as shares or investment property. This has two key implications:

1. Gains are subject to CGT (not income tax at your marginal rate from the first dollar)
2. The **50% CGT discount** applies if you held the asset for more than 12 months before disposal

The exception: if you are running a cryptocurrency business (trading as a business rather than investing), gains may be treated as ordinary income. This is a complex area — most individuals are investors, not traders, under the ATO's tests.

## What Counts as a Disposal?

A **CGT event** occurs each time you:

| Action | CGT Event? |
|---|---|
| Sell crypto for AUD | Yes |
| Trade crypto for another crypto (e.g. BTC → ETH) | Yes |
| Use crypto to buy goods or services | Yes |
| Gift crypto to another person | Yes |
| Receive crypto as salary or payment | Yes (income + later CGT on disposal) |
| Transfer between your own wallets | No |
| Buy crypto with AUD | No |
| HODL (hold without disposing) | No |

## Calculating the Gain or Loss

**Capital gain = Disposal proceeds (AUD value at time of sale) − Cost base**

The **cost base** includes:
- The AUD value at the time you acquired the crypto
- Transaction fees paid to acquire it (exchange fees, gas fees)

**Example — Bitcoin purchased and sold:**
- Bought 0.5 BTC at $40,000 AUD each = $20,000 cost base
- Sold 0.5 BTC at $65,000 AUD each = $32,500 proceeds
- Capital gain = $32,500 − $20,000 = **$12,500**
- Held 14 months → 50% discount applies: **$6,250 taxable gain**

**Example — Crypto-to-crypto trade:**
- Held ETH with cost base of $3,000, now worth $8,000
- Trade all ETH for SOL worth $8,000
- Capital gain = $8,000 − $3,000 = **$5,000** (taxable CGT event despite never touching AUD)
- The SOL now has a cost base of $8,000 (its AUD value at acquisition)

## Staking, Airdrops, and DeFi

These areas are more complex and the ATO has published guidance:

**Staking rewards:** Treated as **ordinary income** at the AUD value when received. When you later sell the staked coins, CGT applies to the gain from the income value at receipt to the sale proceeds.

**Airdrops:** If received as part of a scheme for promotion or as payment for services, taxable as ordinary income. True random airdrops with no conditions may be treated as a CGT event with a cost base of zero.

**DeFi (lending, liquidity pools):** Highly fact-specific. Depositing assets into a liquidity pool may or may not be a CGT event depending on whether you receive a new token in exchange. Interest earned in DeFi protocols is generally ordinary income. This area is still evolving.

**NFTs:** Treated as CGT assets. The same rules apply — disposal triggers CGT.

## Record-Keeping Requirements

The ATO requires you to keep records of every transaction for **five years**. You need:

- Date of each transaction
- AUD value at the time (you must use the market rate — most exchanges record this)
- What you acquired and what you disposed of
- Transaction fees
- Wallet addresses

Most exchanges provide a downloadable transaction history. Third-party crypto tax tools (Koinly, CryptoTaxCalculator, etc.) can import exchange data and generate ATO-compliant reports.

## Losses

Crypto capital losses can be:

- **Offset against crypto or other capital gains** in the same year
- **Carried forward** to future years if no gains to offset this year
- **Not offset against ordinary income** (salary, etc.)

If you hold coins that have permanently lost value or where the project has collapsed, you may be able to claim a capital loss — but you must have records proving the asset is genuinely worthless.

## The ATO's Data Matching

Since 2019, Australian exchanges operating under AUSTRAC regulations are required to report customer data to the ATO, including transaction history and personal identification. The ATO cross-references this against tax returns. If you traded on Coinbase, CoinSpot, Swyftx, or other Australian exchanges and didn't report gains, the ATO likely has the data.

## Putting It Together

Crypto gains increase your taxable income and are taxed at your marginal rate (after the 50% discount if applicable). Use the [tax calculator](/) to see what your marginal rate is — that tells you the tax cost of each dollar of crypto gain. For example, at a 37% marginal rate and a $10,000 taxable gain after the 50% discount, you'll owe approximately **$3,700** in additional tax.
