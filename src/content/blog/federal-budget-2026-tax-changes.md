Budget night, 12 May 2026. Treasurer Jim Chalmers handed down the federal budget, and for most working Australians the headline story is a combination of three changes that all land in the same year: a new standard work deduction, a higher super guarantee, and the usual indexation of HELP repayment thresholds.

None of these are giant surprises — the deduction was pre-announced in April, and the super increase has been legislated for years — but they work together in a way that's worth understanding.

## 1. The $1,000 Standard Work Deduction — Confirmed

The government confirmed that the **$1,000 instant standard deduction** for work-related expenses will proceed as announced. It applies from the **2026–27 income year**, meaning the first tax returns that can use it will be lodged from July 2027.

The mechanics are straightforward:

- Employees can claim $1,000 in work-related expenses with **no receipts, no logbook, no documentation**
- If your actual expenses exceed $1,000, you can still itemise and claim the higher amount
- Charitable donations, union fees, and professional memberships are claimable **on top** of the standard deduction

This replaces the long-standing $300 no-receipt threshold. For someone on a 30% marginal rate, the difference between the old $300 ceiling and the new $1,000 is an extra **$210 in tax savings**.

About 6.2 million workers — 42% of taxpayers — are expected to use it.

## 2. Superannuation Guarantee Rises to 12%

The Superannuation Guarantee rate increases to **12%** from 1 July 2026, up from 11.5% in 2025–26. This is the final step of a legislated schedule that's been in place since 2021.

What this means practically:

- If you earn $80,000, your employer now contributes **$9,600 per year** into your super (up from $9,200)
- If you entered your salary as a **total package** (base + super), your base salary is slightly lower for the same package total
- If you're on a **base salary** arrangement, your take-home pay is unaffected — the extra super comes from your employer on top

For most people, the super increase is a long-term wealth gain rather than an immediate cash boost. But it does affect salary package calculations, especially for higher earners negotiating total remuneration.

## 3. Tax Brackets: Unchanged for 2026–27

The income tax brackets remain the same as 2025–26:

| Taxable Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 – $18,200 | Nil |
| $18,201 – $45,000 | 16% |
| $45,001 – $135,000 | 30% |
| $135,001 – $190,000 | 37% |
| $190,001 and over | 45% |

No changes to the Low Income Tax Offset (LITO), no new brackets, no bracket creep adjustments announced for 2026–27. The Stage 3 structure introduced in 2024–25 holds.

## 4. HELP Repayment Thresholds — Indexed Upward

HECS-HELP repayment thresholds are indexed each year to Average Weekly Earnings. For 2026–27, the entry threshold is expected to rise modestly from the 2025–26 floor of $54,435.

The practical effect: if you're close to the repayment threshold, you may repay slightly less (or nothing at all) in 2026–27 compared to 2025–26 at the same income. Updated official thresholds will be confirmed by the ATO before the 2026–27 year begins.

## 5. Energy Bill Relief Extended

While not a tax measure directly, the budget extended **energy bill rebates** for eligible households. This doesn't change your taxable income or tax liability, but it does affect real disposable income for those who qualify.

## What This All Adds Up To

For a typical full-time employee earning $75,000:

- **$1,000 standard deduction** saves approximately **$300** in income tax (at the 30% marginal rate)
- **Super guarantee at 12%** adds $450 more per year into their retirement savings
- **HELP threshold indexation** (if applicable) marginally reduces their repayment obligation

None of these are transformative on their own. Together they represent a modest but real improvement to the financial position of mid-income workers — which is roughly the population this budget appears to be targeting.

## What's Not Changing

A few things people ask about that were *not* adjusted in this budget:

- **Medicare levy** remains at 2% with the same shade-in rules
- **Medicare Levy Surcharge** thresholds for private health cover unchanged
- **CGT discount and negative gearing** rules untouched (despite pre-budget speculation)
- **Non-resident tax rates** remain as per 2025–26
