The Stage 3 tax cuts took effect from **1 July 2024** (the start of the 2024–25 financial year) and represent the largest restructure of Australia's income tax brackets in decades. The cuts were legislated in 2019 as part of a three-stage reform package and ultimately passed in a modified form after the Albanese government amended the original design in early 2024.

## What Were the Original Stage 3 Cuts?

The original legislation would have:

- Abolished the 32.5% bracket entirely
- Merged the 37% bracket into a single flat 30% rate from $45,001 to $200,000
- Increased the 45% threshold from $180,000 to $200,000

This design was estimated to save high-income earners up to $9,075 per year, while providing smaller savings for those under $120,000.

## What Was Actually Implemented?

In January 2024, the Albanese government redesigned Stage 3 to provide greater relief to low and middle earners. The modified cuts, which took effect from 1 July 2024, are:

**Before Stage 3 (2023–24):**

| Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 – $18,200 | 0% |
| $18,201 – $45,000 | 19% |
| $45,001 – $120,000 | 32.5% |
| $120,001 – $180,000 | 37% |
| $180,001 and above | 45% |

**After Stage 3 (2024–25 onwards):**

| Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 – $18,200 | 0% |
| $18,201 – $45,000 | 16% |
| $45,001 – $135,000 | 30% |
| $135,001 – $190,000 | 37% |
| $190,001 and above | 45% |

## The Four Key Changes

1. **19% → 16%** on $18,201–$45,000: Lowered the second bracket rate by 3 percentage points. This benefits every taxpayer earning above $18,200.

2. **32.5% → 30%** on $45,001–$120,000: The old bracket got a rate cut. Everyone in this band (and above) benefits.

3. **Bracket extended to $135,000:** The 30% bracket was stretched from $120,000 to $135,000. Income between $120,001 and $135,000 previously attracted 37% — it now attracts 30%.

4. **45% threshold moved from $180,000 to $190,000:** High earners get an extra $10,000 at 37% instead of 45%.

## Who Saved What?

The savings compared to 2023–24 rates (resident taxpayers, before LITO and Medicare):

| Salary | 2023–24 Tax | 2024–25 Tax | Annual Saving |
|---|---|---|---|
| $40,000 | $4,142 | $3,488 | **$654** |
| $60,000 | $11,167 | $9,788 | **$1,379** |
| $80,000 | $17,917 | $14,788 | **$3,129** |
| $100,000 | $24,967 | $20,788 | **$4,179** |
| $120,000 | $31,867 | $26,788 | **$5,079** |
| $150,000 | $43,117 | $36,388 | **$6,729** |
| $190,000 | $54,097 | $51,638 | **$2,459** |

*Figures are income tax before LITO; Medicare levy not included. The $190,000 row saves less than $150,000 because the original 37% bracket was already capped and the redesigned cuts redistributed more savings downward.*

## Why Do Lower Earners Benefit More (Relatively)?

The modified design deliberately front-loaded savings. The 3-point cut to the 19% bracket (now 16%) benefits everyone who earns above $18,200 — including the lowest earners. A person on $40,000 saves more as a percentage of their income than someone on $160,000 under the revised design.

Under the original (unmodified) Stage 3, someone on $40,000 would have saved around $500, while someone on $200,000 would have saved $9,075. The revised cuts flipped that relationship significantly.

## The LITO Interaction

The Low Income Tax Offset (LITO) was unchanged at $700 maximum. Because the lower brackets now have lower rates, the tax before LITO is lower — meaning LITO phases out slightly differently in practice, though the schedule itself stayed the same.
