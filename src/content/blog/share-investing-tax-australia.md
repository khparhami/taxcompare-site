Investing in shares is one of the most common ways Australians build wealth outside of superannuation, but many investors — especially beginners — aren't entirely clear on how their share investments are taxed. Here's a comprehensive breakdown.

## Dividends: Assessable Income

When a company pays you a dividend, that dividend is **assessable income** in the year it is paid — regardless of whether you reinvest it or withdraw it. You add it to your other income and it's taxed at your marginal rate.

### Franked Dividends

Most ASX-listed company dividends come with **franking credits** (also called imputation credits) representing the corporate tax already paid. The grossed-up dividend (cash + credits) is included in your assessable income, but the credits offset your tax. See the detailed [franking credits guide](/blog/franking-credits-explained/).

### Unfranked Dividends and International Shares

Dividends from international companies, REITs, and some domestic companies are unfranked — no credits attached. The full cash dividend is assessable income. If foreign withholding tax was deducted (common with US shares — typically 15% under the Australia-US tax treaty), you can claim a **foreign income tax offset** in your return.

### Dividend Reinvestment Plans (DRPs)

If you participate in a DRP, the reinvested amount is still assessable as dividend income in the year received. The shares acquired through DRP have a **cost base equal to the price used** in the plan — keep records of every DRP acquisition because you'll need them when you eventually sell.

## Capital Gains Tax on Shares

When you sell shares for more than you paid, you make a capital gain. This gain is added to your taxable income and taxed at your marginal rate.

**Capital gain = Sale proceeds (less brokerage) − Cost base (purchase price plus brokerage)**

### The 50% CGT Discount

If you held the shares for **more than 12 months**, you can apply the **50% CGT discount** — only half the gain is taxable.

**Example:**
- Bought 1,000 shares at $5.00 each (cost base: $5,000 + $20 brokerage = $5,020)
- Sold 18 months later at $8.00 each ($8,000 − $20 brokerage = $7,980)
- Capital gain: $7,980 − $5,020 = $2,960
- After 50% discount: **$1,480 taxable gain**

### Capital Losses

If you sell shares for less than cost base, you make a capital loss. Losses offset gains in the same year or carry forward to future years. They cannot offset salary or other ordinary income.

**Tax-loss harvesting:** Deliberately selling loss-making shares to offset gains elsewhere is a legitimate strategy — commonly done before 30 June.

## ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)

ETFs are taxed similarly to shares, but have some nuances:

- **Distributions** from ETFs may include dividends, capital gains components, and return of capital — each is treated differently
- **International ETFs** (like Vanguard World or iShares S&P 500) may have foreign income, withholding taxes, and complex distribution statements
- **Annual tax statements** from your ETF provider (usually sent by late September) detail each component — don't rely on the cash distribution alone

Most brokers provide a combined tax report for all ETF distributions. Check it against your records.

## Managed Funds

Managed funds distribute income annually, usually in June or July. Even if you don't withdraw from the fund, you must declare the distributed income. The distribution statement breaks down:

- Australian dividends (with franking credits)
- Interest
- Net capital gains (already net of losses within the fund)
- Foreign income
- Return of capital (reduces your cost base — not assessable income)

## Records You Need to Keep

For **every** share transaction:
- Date purchased and date sold
- Number of shares
- Purchase price and sale price
- Brokerage paid on purchase and sale
- For DRP: the price per share at each reinvestment

For **dividends and distributions:**
- Date paid
- Cash amount
- Franking credit amount
- Any foreign withholding tax

Keep these records for **five years after the relevant return is lodged**. For shares you hold long-term, that means keeping purchase records for years or decades.

## Wash Sales Warning

The ATO watches for **wash sale arrangements** — selling shares to crystallise a loss and immediately repurchasing the same shares. If the dominant purpose is obtaining a tax benefit (not genuinely exiting the investment), the ATO can deny the loss. Wait a reasonable period before repurchasing, and make sure the decision to sell is commercially genuine.

## Shares Within Super vs Outside Super

The tax treatment inside super is far more favourable:

| | Outside super (own name) | Inside super (accumulation) | Inside super (pension) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dividends taxed at | Marginal rate | 15% | 0% |
| Capital gains (12+ months) | Marginal rate after 50% discount | 10% | 0% |
| Capital gains (under 12 months) | Marginal rate | 15% | 0% |

This is why high-income earners typically prioritise maxing out super contributions for share investing where possible.

## Putting It in Your Return

All share income is reported in your annual tax return:
- Dividends and distributions: income section, supported by a prefilled tax statement in myTax
- Capital gains: capital gains schedule (Schedule 3)

myTax pre-fills data for most ASX shares and large ETFs from company and registry data. International shares and smaller ETFs may need manual entry.
