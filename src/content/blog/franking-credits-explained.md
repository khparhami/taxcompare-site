Franking credits are one of Australia's most distinctive tax features — and one of the most valuable for share investors. They can reduce your tax bill, and in some cases generate a cash refund from the ATO even if you owe no income tax. Here's how they work.

## The Problem Franking Credits Solve

Without franking credits, company profits would be taxed twice:

1. The company pays **30% corporate tax** on its profits
2. The shareholder then pays **income tax** on the dividend received

Australia's **dividend imputation** system prevents this double taxation. When a company pays tax on its profits and distributes those profits as dividends, it can "frank" the dividend with a credit representing the tax already paid. Shareholders use that credit to offset their own tax liability.

## How Franking Credits Work

When you receive a franked dividend:

1. The **cash dividend** arrives in your account
2. A **franking credit** (also called an imputation credit) is attached, representing tax the company already paid
3. Both the cash dividend and the franking credit are included in your taxable income
4. You then claim the franking credit as a tax offset against your income tax bill

The grossed-up dividend (cash + credit) is included in your assessable income, but the credit offsets the tax on it.

## The Maths

Australian companies pay 30% corporate tax. For every $70 of after-tax profit distributed as a cash dividend, $30 in company tax was paid — creating a $30 franking credit on a $100 gross dividend.

**Franking credit = (Cash dividend ÷ (1 − corporate tax rate)) × corporate tax rate**

For a fully franked $700 cash dividend:
- Grossed-up income = $700 ÷ 0.70 = **$1,000**
- Franking credit = $1,000 − $700 = **$300**
- Your assessable income includes $1,000
- You claim a $300 tax offset

## The Tax Outcome Depends on Your Marginal Rate

| Your marginal rate | Tax on $1,000 gross dividend | Franking credit | Net tax effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0% (below tax-free threshold) | $0 | $300 credit | **$300 refund** |
| 16% | $160 | $300 credit | **$140 refund** |
| 30% | $300 | $300 credit | **$0 (break even)** |
| 37% | $370 | $300 credit | **$70 additional tax** |
| 45% | $450 | $300 credit | **$150 additional tax** |

The break-even point is the corporate tax rate. At 30% marginal rate, franked dividends are effectively tax-neutral. Below 30%, they generate a refund. Above 30%, some additional tax is owed.

## Partially Franked and Unfranked Dividends

Not all dividends are fully franked. Companies that don't pay Australian corporate tax on all their profits (e.g. those with significant foreign income or tax losses) may pay dividends that are:

- **Fully franked** — franked at the full 30% corporate rate
- **Partially franked** — e.g. 50% franked (credit represents only half the corporate rate)
- **Unfranked** — no franking credit; the full cash amount is taxable income

Unfranked dividends are common from real estate investment trusts (REITs), international companies listed in Australia, and companies with significant tax losses.

## Refundable Franking Credits — A Unique Australian Feature

Since 2000, franking credits have been **fully refundable** in Australia. This means if your franking credits exceed your total tax liability, the ATO pays you the difference as a cash refund.

This is particularly valuable for:

- **Retirees with low income** — a retiree with $30,000 in fully franked dividends and no other income may receive a substantial cash refund
- **Superannuation funds in pension phase** — earnings are tax-free, so franking credits generate full refunds
- **Low-income investors** — those below the tax-free threshold receive refunds on all franking credits

## Claiming Franking Credits

Franking credits are reported in your annual tax return. Your broker or share registry sends an annual tax statement showing dividends received and the attached franking credits. This is pre-filled in myTax for most investors with Australian shares.

## Small Companies and the Lower Corporate Rate

Companies with annual turnover under $50 million may qualify for the lower **25% corporate tax rate** (base rate entities). Their franking credits are calculated at 25%, not 30%.

**Example:** A $700 cash dividend from a base rate entity:
- Gross-up: $700 ÷ 0.75 = $933
- Franking credit: $933 − $700 = **$233**

## Share Investors and Their Tax Return

If you own Australian shares paying dividends, your tax return should include:

- Total dividends received (cash)
- Total franking credits
- The grossed-up dividend amount (included automatically in myTax)

The ATO has pre-filled data from companies and registries, but always check it against your own records — data can be missing or delayed.
