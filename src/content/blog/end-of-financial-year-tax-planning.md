30 June is the hard cutoff for the Australian financial year. Actions taken on 1 July count for next year's return — but actions taken before 30 June can meaningfully reduce this year's tax bill. Here's what to prioritise.

## 1. Top Up Your Super (Concessional Contributions)

This is the single most impactful move for most workers. Concessional (pre-tax) contributions to super are taxed at 15% inside the fund — far below most people's marginal rate.

**The cap:** $30,000 per year, including your employer's SGC contributions.

**How to check your remaining cap:** Log into myGov → ATO → Super → Carry forward concessional contributions. The ATO shows your year-to-date contributions and your available cap.

**How to contribute:** Ask your employer about salary sacrifice before 30 June, or make a personal contribution and lodge a Notice of Intent to Claim a Deduction form with your fund.

**The saving:** At a 30% marginal rate, contributing an extra $5,000 before 30 June saves **$750** in tax (30% − 15% contributions tax × $5,000). At 37%, it's $1,100.

**Carry-forward contributions:** If your total super balance was under $500,000 on 30 June of the prior year, you can use unused cap space from the previous five years. This allows catch-up contributions — sometimes very large ones — in a single high-income year.

## 2. Prepay Deductible Expenses

The general rule: if you incur and pay an expense before 30 June, it's deductible this year (not next).

**Works well for:**
- Prepaying up to 12 months of investment loan interest (for investors with negatively geared properties)
- Income protection insurance premium for the coming year
- Subscriptions, professional memberships renewed before 30 June
- Work-related tools and equipment under $300 (claim immediately)

**The limit:** You can only prepay expenses that cover up to 12 months ahead. Prepaying 24 months' interest only gives you 12 months' deduction this year; the balance sits over.

## 3. Crystallise Capital Losses

If you hold investments sitting at a loss, selling before 30 June lets you offset those losses against capital gains made this year — reducing your CGT bill.

**Example:** You have $15,000 in capital gains from selling shares. You also hold crypto down $8,000. Selling the crypto before 30 June creates a $8,000 capital loss that offsets the share gain, reducing your taxable capital gains to $7,000.

**The trap to avoid:** The ATO's **wash sale rules** mean you cannot sell an asset, claim the loss, and immediately repurchase the same (or substantially similar) asset with the intent of creating an artificial loss. There's no defined "window" in Australian law (unlike the US's 30-day wash sale rule), but the ATO can treat wash sales as schemes and deny the loss if the transaction lacks commercial substance.

## 4. Time Asset Sales Carefully

If you're planning to sell an appreciating asset (shares, property) and you acquired it less than 12 months ago, waiting until after the 12-month anniversary qualifies you for the **50% CGT discount**. If the anniversary falls in July, waiting a few weeks can halve your CGT on the gain.

Conversely, if you're in a high-income year and expect lower income next year (parental leave, sabbatical, retirement), deferring a sale until after 30 June shifts the CGT into the lower-income year.

## 5. Make Charitable Donations

Donations of **$2 or more to registered DGRs** (Deductible Gift Recipients) are tax-deductible. They reduce your taxable income in the year the donation is made — so a donation on 29 June reduces this year's bill; a donation on 1 July reduces next year's.

The ATO's DGR register is searchable at abr.gov.au to confirm eligibility before donating.

## 6. Review Your Work-Related Expenses

Before 30 June, buy any work equipment, tools, or subscriptions you genuinely need and would have bought anyway. Items costing $300 or less with work-related use can be claimed immediately. More expensive items are depreciated but still start generating a deduction this year.

Common last-minute purchases:
- A replacement work phone or tablet
- Software subscriptions (annual plans)
- Professional books or online courses
- Safety equipment or protective gear

## 7. Check Your PAYG Withholding

Review your last few payslips. If your employer has been withholding significantly more than necessary (due to a salary increase part-way through the year, for example), you may be able to adjust your withholding via a Withholding Declaration to stop over-withholding for the remaining weeks.

Alternatively, if you owe tax (investment income, side income) and want to avoid a bill plus interest, you can ask your employer to withhold extra now.

## 8. Low-Income Super Tax Offset (LISTO)

If your income is under $37,000, the government automatically deposits the LISTO (up to $500) into your super after you lodge. This is automatic — but you need to have made or received super contributions during the year to be eligible.

## 9. Government Co-Contribution

If your income is under $43,445 (2024–25), the government will co-contribute 50 cents for every $1 of **after-tax** personal super contributions you make, up to a maximum $500 co-contribution. The co-contribution phases out between $43,445 and $58,445.

Make your after-tax contribution before 30 June and lodge your return — the ATO calculates and deposits the co-contribution automatically.

## Key Dates to Remember

| Date | Action |
|---|---|
| By 30 June | Make super contributions, sell loss-making assets, prepay expenses, donate to DGRs |
| By 31 July | Receive income statement from employer (replaces payment summary) |
| By 31 October | Deadline to lodge your tax return yourself via myTax |
| After lodging | ATO issues notice of assessment (usually within 2 weeks via myTax) |
