Millions of Australians carry a HECS-HELP debt from university, yet many are surprised when their take-home pay is lower than a colleague on the same salary — simply because of a compulsory repayment triggered by their income. Here's exactly how it works.

## What Is HECS-HELP?

**HECS** (Higher Education Contribution Scheme) was the original name; it is now part of the broader **HELP** (Higher Education Loan Program) scheme. When you study at a Commonwealth-supported place, the government covers your tuition upfront and you repay it through the tax system once your income is high enough.

Your debt is indexed to CPI each year on 1 June, so it grows in real terms if you don't repay it quickly.

## When Do Repayments Start?

Repayments kick in once your **repayment income** exceeds the minimum threshold — currently **$54,435** for 2024–25 and 2025–26. Below this figure you pay nothing extra, regardless of how large your debt is.

Repayment income is broadly your taxable income plus reportable fringe benefits, total net investment losses, and reportable employer super contributions. For most salaried employees it is essentially the same as gross salary.

## The Repayment Rates

The critical thing to understand: the repayment rate applies to your **entire** repayment income, not just the portion above the threshold. This creates noticeable jumps at each threshold.

| Repayment Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| Below $54,435 | Nil |
| $54,435 – $62,848 | 1.0% |
| $62,849 – $70,282 | 2.0% |
| $70,283 – $77,780 | 2.5% |
| $77,781 – $87,594 | 3.0% |
| $87,595 – $98,398 | 3.5% |
| $98,399 – $107,112 | 4.0% |
| $107,113 – $121,664 | 4.5% |
| $121,665 – $138,007 | 5.0% |
| $138,008 – $155,584 | 5.5% |
| $155,585 – $166,945 | 6.0% |
| $166,946 – $184,149 | 6.5% |
| $184,150 – $195,873 | 7.0% |
| $195,874 – $214,127 | 7.5% |
| $214,128 and above | 10.0% |

*Thresholds are CPI-indexed annually.*

## Real-Money Impact

Here's what HECS repayments look like at common salary levels in 2025–26:

| Salary | Rate | Annual Repayment | Monthly Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| $55,000 | 1.0% | $550 | −$46 |
| $70,000 | 2.0% | $1,400 | −$117 |
| $80,000 | 3.0% | $2,400 | −$200 |
| $100,000 | 4.0% | $4,000 | −$333 |
| $120,000 | 4.5% | $5,400 | −$450 |
| $150,000 | 5.5% | $8,250 | −$688 |

## The Threshold Cliff — A Watch-Out

Because the rate applies to total income, crossing a threshold costs you more than it saves. Consider:

- At **$62,848** you repay 1.0% × $62,848 = **$628**
- At **$62,849** you repay 2.0% × $62,849 = **$1,257** — an extra **$629** for earning one more dollar

This doesn't mean you should avoid earning more — you still come out ahead overall — but it's worth understanding when you're close to a threshold.

## How Your Employer Handles It

When you start a job, your Tax File Number (TFN) declaration includes a question about HELP debt. If you tick "yes", your employer uses a higher withholding schedule to collect extra throughout the year. If you forget to tick it, you'll face a lump-sum repayment at tax time.

## Voluntary Repayments

You can make voluntary repayments at any time via MyGov or directly to the ATO. There is no bonus for doing so (the previous 5% discount was scrapped in 2017), but paying down the debt reduces the principal before 1 June indexation is applied.

If your income is just above a threshold and you expect to drop back below it (e.g. parental leave, part-year work), you can apply to the ATO to have your withholding adjusted so you're not over-withheld during the year.

## When Does the Debt End?

Your HELP debt is cancelled if you:

- Pay it off in full through compulsory and/or voluntary repayments
- Reach the age of 65 (in most circumstances) without paying it off — the remaining balance is waived
- Die — the debt does not pass to your estate

There is no fixed repayment period or minimum monthly amount outside the annual compulsory schedule.
