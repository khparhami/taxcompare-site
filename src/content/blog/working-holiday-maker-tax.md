Australia hosts hundreds of thousands of working holiday makers each year on 417 (Working Holiday) and 462 (Work and Holiday) visas. Since 2017, these workers have been subject to a special tax rate — separate from both the resident and standard non-resident tax scales.

## The Working Holiday Maker Tax Rate

Working holiday makers are taxed at a flat **15%** on the first $45,000 of income earned in Australia. Above $45,000, regular non-resident marginal rates apply.

| Taxable Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 – $45,000 | 15% (flat, from dollar one) |
| $45,001 – $135,000 | 32.5% |
| $135,001 – $190,000 | 37% |
| $190,001 and above | 45% |

There is **no tax-free threshold** for working holiday makers — the 15% rate applies from the first dollar earned.

**Example:** A working holiday maker earning $40,000 in Australia pays:
- $40,000 × 15% = **$6,000 income tax**

A resident earning the same would pay:
- $0 (tax-free threshold) + ($40,000 − $18,200) × 16% = **$3,488** (plus LITO reducing this further)

Working holiday makers pay significantly more tax at lower income levels.

## No LITO, No Medicare Levy

Working holiday makers are:
- **Not eligible** for the Low Income Tax Offset (LITO)
- **Not subject** to the Medicare levy (they are not Australian residents)
- **Not eligible** to claim the HECS tax-free threshold (HELP debt is irrelevant unless they have an existing Australian HELP debt)

## Employer Registration Requirement

Employers who hire working holiday makers must **register with the ATO** as a "working holiday maker employer" before making the first payment. An employer who fails to register must withhold tax at the higher 32.5% non-resident rate instead of 15%.

If your employer is withholding 32.5% or 47% (which happens if you haven't provided your TFN), you may be able to claim a refund when you lodge.

## Tax File Number (TFN)

Working holiday makers should apply for a Tax File Number (TFN) before starting work. Without one, employers must withhold **47%**. TFN applications can be made online at ato.gov.au or at an Australia Post branch with appropriate identity documents.

## Superannuation Entitlements

Working holiday makers **are entitled to super** under the same rules as other employees. If you earn $450 or more per month from a single employer (the $450 threshold has been removed as of 1 July 2022 — all earnings now attract super), your employer must pay SGC contributions (12% in 2025–26) to your chosen fund.

### Claiming Your Super Back When You Leave

When you depart Australia permanently (or your visa expires and you don't return), you can claim your super as a **Departing Australia Superannuation Payment (DASP)**.

The DASP is taxed at a flat **35%** for working holiday makers (this rate was increased from 38% for most WHMs; check the current ATO rate before claiming).

To make a DASP claim, apply online through the ATO's DASP application service after departing. The process typically takes 28 days.

## Lodging a Tax Return

Working holiday makers who earned income in Australia generally need to lodge a tax return. However:

- You lodge as a **working holiday maker** tax status (not resident or non-resident)
- If you only earned income from an employer who correctly withheld at 15%, and had no other income or deductions, you may receive a small refund or owe nothing
- The lodgement deadline is **31 October** (or you must file before departing if leaving before then)

Working holiday makers **can** claim work-related deductions — the same rules apply as for other taxpayers. This includes vehicle expenses, tools, protective clothing, and work from home if applicable.

## Tax Treaties

Australia has tax treaties with many countries that may affect your obligations in your home country. The Australian tax you pay on Australian earnings generally qualifies as a foreign tax credit in most treaty partner countries. Check with a tax professional in your home country.

## Comparing to Regular Non-Resident Rates

| Income | Working Holiday Maker | Standard Non-Resident |
|---|---|---|
| $20,000 | 15% = $3,000 | 30% = $6,000 |
| $40,000 | 15% = $6,000 | 30% = $12,000 |
| $45,000 | 15% = $6,750 | 30% = $13,500 |
| $60,000 | $6,750 + 32.5% on $15,000 = $11,625 | 30% = $18,000 |

The 15% rate is substantially cheaper than the standard non-resident 30% rate, making the working holiday maker tax status beneficial relative to the alternative.
