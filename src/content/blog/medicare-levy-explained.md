The Medicare levy is one of the simpler parts of the Australian tax system — it's mostly just 2% of your taxable income — but there are exemptions, a phase-in zone for low earners, and a separate surcharge that can catch high-income earners without private hospital cover off guard.

## What Is the Medicare Levy?

The Medicare levy funds Australia's universal public health care system. It is collected through the same PAYG system as income tax and appears as a separate line item on your tax assessment each year.

For most Australian residents the levy is a flat **2%** of taxable income. A person on $80,000 pays **$1,600** in Medicare levy on top of their income tax.

## Who Pays It?

You pay the Medicare levy if you are:

- An Australian resident for tax purposes
- Above the low-income threshold for the year

You are **exempt** if you are:

- A **foreign resident** (non-residents pay higher income tax rates instead and owe no Medicare levy)
- A **low-income earner** below the threshold (you may pay a reduced amount in the shade-in zone)
- Entitled to a reduction because your taxable income is very low and you meet specific criteria

## The Low-Income Shade-In Zone

Rather than a sudden 2% levy appearing once income crosses the threshold, a **shade-in** rule applies. In the shade-in zone, the levy equals 10% of the excess above the threshold — climbing gradually until it equals the full 2% of total income.

For **2025–26 and 2024–25**:

| Income | Medicare Levy |
|---|---|
| Below $26,000 | Nil |
| $26,001 – ~$32,500 | 10% of the amount over $26,000 (shade-in zone) |
| Above ~$32,500 | 2% of total income |

For **2023–24**:

| Income | Medicare Levy |
|---|---|
| Below $24,276 | Nil |
| $24,277 – ~$30,345 | 10% of the amount over $24,276 (shade-in zone) |
| Above ~$30,345 | 2% of total income |

**How the shade-in endpoint is calculated:** The full levy kicks in when the shade-in amount (10% of excess) equals 2% of the full income. Solving: `(income − threshold) × 0.10 = income × 0.02` gives `income = threshold / 0.8`.

## Families and Dependent Children

Low-income families with dependants have a higher threshold before the levy applies. The family threshold rises for each dependent child or student. If you're a sole parent or one of a couple with a family income below the threshold, you may be entitled to a reduction — check the ATO's Medicare levy reduction calculator for your specific circumstances.

## The Medicare Levy Surcharge (MLS)

The MLS is a **separate, additional charge** on top of the 2% levy. It applies to:

- Singles with taxable income above **$93,000**
- Couples/families with combined income above **$186,000**

who do **not** hold an appropriate level of private hospital cover.

| Income | MLS Rate |
|---|---|
| $93,001 – $108,000 | 1.0% |
| $108,001 – $144,000 | 1.25% |
| $144,001 and above | 1.5% |

The MLS is designed to encourage higher earners to take out private hospital insurance, reducing pressure on the public system. If your insurance premium is less than the surcharge you'd pay, private cover is financially worthwhile — and vice versa.

**Note:** The base calculator on this site includes the standard 2% Medicare levy but does not include the Medicare Levy Surcharge, as entitlement depends on whether you hold qualifying private hospital cover.

## Practical Examples

**$50,000 salary (2025–26):** Well above the shade-in zone. Medicare levy = $50,000 × 2% = **$1,000**.

**$28,000 salary (2025–26):** In the shade-in zone. Medicare levy = ($28,000 − $26,000) × 10% = **$200**.

**$22,000 salary (2025–26):** Below the $26,000 threshold. Medicare levy = **$0**.

**$100,000 salary, no private hospital cover (2025–26):** Medicare levy = $2,000. No MLS because income is below $93,000 surcharge threshold. *(Wait — $100,000 is above $93,000, so MLS of 1.0% = $1,000 also applies, for a total health-system contribution of $3,000.)*
