Owning a rental property comes with tax obligations and tax opportunities in equal measure. All rental income must be declared, but landlords can offset substantial expenses — and in many cases, a net rental loss reduces their overall tax bill. Here's a complete guide.

## What Rental Income Must Be Declared?

**All rental income received** during the financial year is assessable, including:

- Regular weekly or monthly rent
- Bond money you keep (if the tenant forfeits it)
- Insurance payouts for lost rent
- Reimbursements from tenants for expenses you paid
- Rent from renting out a room in your home (even informally)
- Income from short-term letting (Airbnb, Stayz, VRBO)

Rent received for a period before you receive it (rent paid in advance) is generally assessable when received, not when the period it covers occurs.

## When Is the Property Available for Rent?

You can only claim deductions for periods when the property was **genuinely available for rent**. If the property was vacant because you were using it for personal purposes, or if you set rent at an artificially low rate for a family member, deductions are limited or unavailable for that period.

**Holiday homes** attract particular ATO scrutiny — if you use the property personally and also rent it out, you must apportion deductions based on the proportion of time it was genuinely available for rent at market rates.

## Immediately Deductible Expenses

These costs are deductible in the financial year they are incurred:

| Expense | Notes |
|---|---|
| Mortgage interest (investment loan) | Only interest, not principal repayments |
| Property management fees | Agent commissions, letting fees |
| Council rates | General rates, not levies for improvements |
| Water rates | Where you pay (not charged to tenant) |
| Land tax | Deductible in the state where it's levied |
| Building and contents insurance | Full premium |
| Landlord insurance | Covers loss of rent, tenant damage |
| Repairs and maintenance | Restoring existing items to original condition |
| Gardening and lawn mowing | If not the tenant's responsibility |
| Pest control and cleaning | Between tenancies |
| Advertising for tenants | Online listings, property management marketing |
| Stationery, postage, phone calls | Relating to managing the property |
| Quantity surveyor fees | For preparing a depreciation schedule |
| Body corporate/strata fees | Annual levies for common area maintenance |

**Repairs vs improvements — an important distinction:**
- **Repairs** restore an existing asset to its original condition → immediately deductible
- **Improvements** enhance or extend the asset beyond its original condition → capital and depreciated over time

Replacing a broken fence with a similar fence = repair. Replacing a broken fence with a substantially better one = improvement.

Initial repairs to a property you've just purchased (fixing pre-existing damage) are **not** immediately deductible — they are treated as capital improvements.

## Deductions Over Time: Depreciation

Two categories of depreciation are available on investment properties:

### Division 43 — Building Allowance

A 2.5% per year deduction on the **original construction cost** of the building. This applies to buildings constructed after 18 July 1985 (most modern properties). If the original construction cost was $300,000, you can claim $7,500 per year in building allowance.

You don't need receipts for the original build — a quantity surveyor can estimate construction costs for older properties.

### Division 40 — Plant and Equipment

Depreciating assets within the property — appliances, hot water systems, carpet, blinds, air conditioners, smoke alarms, and so on — are depreciated over their effective life at either the prime cost or diminishing value method.

**Post-May 2017 restriction:** If you purchased a residential property after 9 May 2017, you can only claim depreciation on plant and equipment for assets that are **new at the time you buy them** or that you install yourself after purchase. Existing second-hand assets (the previous owner's carpet, oven, etc.) cannot be depreciated by the new buyer.

New properties, or properties where you install new appliances and fittings, are unaffected by this restriction.

### Quantity Surveyor Schedule

A depreciation schedule from a registered quantity surveyor (typically $500–$900, itself tax-deductible) itemises every depreciable asset and calculates the maximum annual deductions. For new properties or those with significant fit-outs, the schedule typically pays for itself many times over.

## Short-Term Letting (Airbnb)

Renting your property (or a room) on Airbnb or similar platforms:

- All income is assessable
- Deductions are available for the proportion of the year the property was available for rent (not personally used)
- If it's your main residence for part of the year and you rent it out for part of the year, deductions are proportional
- The CGT main residence exemption may be partially lost for the rental period

Platforms report data to the ATO — undeclaring Airbnb income is high-risk.

## Rental Summary in the Tax Return

Your rental property details are entered on the **rental schedule** in your tax return:
- Total rent received
- Each deductible expense category
- Depreciation (from your quantity surveyor schedule)
- Net rental income or loss

If the result is a net loss (negative gearing), it is deducted from your total assessable income — reducing your overall taxable income and tax bill. See the [negative gearing guide](/blog/negative-gearing-explained/) for a full worked example.

## Records to Keep

Maintain records for each property:
- Rent receipts and bank records of rental income
- All expense receipts (rates, insurance, repairs, agent statements)
- Depreciation schedule and supporting documents
- Loan statements showing interest paid
- Quantity surveyor report

**Retention period:** Five years from the date you lodge the relevant return, and five years after you sell the property (for CGT records).
