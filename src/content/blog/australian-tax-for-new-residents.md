Whether you've arrived on a skilled visa, a partner visa, or permanent residency, the Australian tax system has rules you need to understand from your first day of employment. Getting set up correctly from the start avoids problems — and potentially large tax bills — later.

## Step 1: Determine Your Tax Residency Status

Australia uses its own "tax residency" rules that are separate from visa status or immigration residency. You can be a tax resident from day one of arriving in Australia if you intend to reside here.

The primary test for migrants is the **resides test**: do you live in Australia on an ongoing basis? If you've moved here with the intention of staying (not a brief visit), you are almost certainly a tax resident from arrival.

**Why this matters:**
- **Residents** pay income tax on worldwide income, get the tax-free threshold ($18,200), access LITO, and pay the Medicare levy
- **Non-residents** pay tax only on Australian income, at higher flat rates, with no tax-free threshold and no LITO or Medicare levy

For most new migrants arriving to live and work, you're a tax resident. If your situation is complex (partial year, business activity in multiple countries), seek advice.

## Step 2: Apply for a Tax File Number (TFN)

A Tax File Number is your personal identifier with the ATO. You need one to:

- Work legally (without one, your employer withholds 47%)
- Earn interest on Australian bank accounts (without it, 47% withholding)
- Lodge a tax return
- Access super

**How to apply:**
- Foreign passport holders can apply online at ato.gov.au once they arrive in Australia
- The ATO verifies your identity against Department of Home Affairs records
- Processing typically takes 28 days — apply as soon as you arrive

Your TFN is permanent and yours for life, regardless of visa changes or citizenship.

## Step 3: Set Up Superannuation

If you're employed, your employer must pay **12% of your ordinary time earnings** into a super fund from your first pay (no waiting period for new employees since the $450 monthly threshold was removed in July 2022).

**Choosing a fund:**
- If you don't choose a fund, your employer uses their default fund
- You can nominate any APRA-regulated fund, including industry funds (AustralianSuper, REST, Hostplus, etc.) and retail funds
- Open a fund account online — most take 10 minutes

**Keep your TFN with your fund:** Providing your TFN to your super fund ensures you can access government contributions and prevents higher tax inside the fund.

## Step 4: Complete a TFN Declaration for Your Employer

When you start a new job, complete a **TFN Declaration** form telling your employer:

- Your TFN
- Whether you're an Australian resident for tax purposes (**tick yes** if you are — see Step 1)
- Whether you're claiming the tax-free threshold (yes, unless you have another job)
- Whether you have a HELP debt (most new migrants don't, but relevant if you've studied in Australia before)

## The Tax-Free Threshold — Claim It

As a tax resident, you're entitled to the **$18,200 tax-free threshold** — meaning no income tax on your first $18,200. Claim this from your first employer by ticking the box on the TFN Declaration. You can only claim it from one employer at a time.

## The 183-Day Rule — A Common Misconception

Some migrants believe they need to live in Australia for 183 days before becoming a tax resident. This is partially accurate but commonly misapplied:

- The **183-day test** is a secondary test for individuals who are not permanent migrants — it applies to people on short-term visits or business trips
- If you've moved to Australia on a permanent or long-term visa with the intention to reside here, you become a tax resident on **arrival** — not after 183 days

This distinction matters because a non-resident in the first 183 days would miss the tax-free threshold and LITO.

## Worldwide Income

As an Australian tax resident, you must declare **all worldwide income** in your Australian tax return, including:

- Salary or business income earned overseas before arriving
- Interest from foreign bank accounts
- Rent from property you own abroad
- Foreign pension payments

Australia has tax treaties with many countries to prevent double taxation. Foreign tax paid can usually be claimed as a **foreign income tax offset** in your Australian return.

## Your First Tax Return

The Australian financial year runs **1 July to 30 June**. Your first return covers whatever portion of that year you were in Australia.

For example, if you arrived on 1 February 2026, your first return (due 31 October 2026) covers 1 February to 30 June 2026 — five months of income.

Because your income is pro-rated but your tax brackets are applied to the annualised amount (by your employer's withholding), you may receive a refund for being over-withheld during a partial year.

## New Residents and the Medicare Levy

As a tax resident you'll pay the 2% Medicare levy — you're entitled to use Medicare from arrival if you come from a country with a reciprocal healthcare agreement (UK, Ireland, New Zealand, and many others). Residents from countries without a treaty still pay the levy but have limited Medicare access initially.
