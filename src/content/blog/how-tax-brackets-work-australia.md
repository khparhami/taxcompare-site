"I didn't want that bonus — it'll just push me into a higher tax bracket."

This is one of the most persistent myths in Australian personal finance. The fear of a higher tax bracket is understandable, but it's based on a misunderstanding of how progressive taxation actually works. A higher bracket rate never applies to your whole income — only to the dollars that fall within that bracket.

## The Staircase, Not the Cliff

Think of Australia's tax system as a staircase. Each step represents a bracket, and the rate only applies to income sitting on that particular step.

For **2025–26 residents**, the staircase looks like this:

| Step | Income Range | Rate on This Step |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | $0 – $18,200 | 0% |
| 2 | $18,201 – $45,000 | 16% |
| 3 | $45,001 – $135,000 | 30% |
| 4 | $135,001 – $190,000 | 37% |
| 5 | $190,001+ | 45% |

When your income increases and pushes into the next bracket, **only the additional income** is taxed at the new rate. Everything below the threshold is unaffected.

## A Worked Example

**You earn $44,000. Your boss offers a $5,000 raise.**

Before the raise:
- Steps 1 and 2 apply
- Tax = $0 + ($44,000 − $18,200) × 16% = **$4,128**

After the raise ($49,000):
- Steps 1, 2, and 3 apply
- Tax = $0 + ($45,000 − $18,200) × 16% + ($49,000 − $45,000) × 30%
- Tax = $4,288 + $1,200 = **$5,488**

The extra $5,000 in income cost **$1,360** in extra tax — leaving you **$3,640 better off**, even though you crossed into the 30% bracket.

You are always better off earning more. The bracket rate applies to the marginal dollar, not every dollar.

## Why the Myth Persists

The confusion usually comes from looking at **average tax rates** rather than **marginal rates**.

If you earn $100,000 and pay $20,788 in tax, some people calculate: $20,788 ÷ $100,000 = 20.8% — that's your **effective** (average) rate. But your **marginal** rate is 30%. The average rate sounds much lower because the first $45,000 was taxed at only 0–16%.

People sometimes confuse their marginal rate for their effective rate, assume all their income is taxed at 30%, and conclude that earning more won't help.

## The Marginal Rate vs Effective Rate Distinction

| Concept | Definition | Example at $90,000 |
|---|---|---|
| **Marginal rate** | Rate on your next dollar earned | 30% |
| **Effective rate** | Total tax ÷ total income | ~21.8% |

The marginal rate tells you how much of any *extra* earnings you keep. The effective rate tells you what percentage of your *total* income went to tax.

Both are useful — but they answer different questions.

## What Changing Brackets Actually Means for You

When you're near a bracket threshold, here's the real picture:

**At $134,999 (just under the 37% threshold):**
- Marginal rate: 30%
- Extra $1 earned: you keep 70 cents

**At $135,001 (just over the 37% threshold):**
- Marginal rate: 37%
- Extra $1 earned: you keep 63 cents
- But your previous income is completely unaffected

A $2 income increase around this threshold costs you an extra 7 cents on just those 2 dollars — not thousands.

## The Low Income Tax Offset Interplay

There's one area where an income increase genuinely can reduce your net position: the **phase-out zones of tax offsets**. For example, the LITO reduces by 5c per dollar between $37,500 and $45,000. In this band:

- You pay 16% income tax on extra income
- Plus you lose 5c of LITO per dollar
- **Effective marginal rate = 21%** (16% + 5%)

This isn't a trap — you're still better off — but your effective marginal rate is higher than the nominal bracket rate. The same applies at $45,001–$66,667 where LITO phases out at 1.5c per dollar (31.5% effective marginal rate).

## The Real Tax Threshold to Watch

There is one true "cliff" to be aware of: the **HELP repayment threshold** ($54,435 in 2025–26). Once your income crosses this point, HECS repayments begin — and the rate applies to your entire income, not just the excess. Crossing from $54,434 to $54,435 costs you 1.0% of $54,435 = $544 in repayments. That said, you still keep $54,435 − ($54,435 × 1%) = $53,891, versus $54,434 below the threshold — still ahead by $1,457.
