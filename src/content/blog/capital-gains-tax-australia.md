Capital Gains Tax (CGT) is not a separate tax in Australia — it's part of your income tax. When you sell a capital asset for more than you paid for it, the profit (the capital gain) is added to your taxable income and taxed at your marginal rate. Understanding how it works — and the discounts available — can make a significant difference to your after-tax return.

## What Assets Are Subject to CGT?

CGT applies to most assets acquired after 20 September 1985, including:

- **Shares and managed funds** — Australian and foreign
- **Investment property** — residential and commercial
- **Cryptocurrency** — each disposal is a CGT event
- **Collectables** worth over $500 (art, jewellery, coins)
- **Personal use assets** worth over $10,000
- **Business goodwill**

## What Is Exempt?

Key CGT exemptions include:

- **Your main residence** — the home you live in is generally exempt (with conditions around partial use or extended vacancy)
- **Cars and motorcycles** — personal vehicles
- **Personal use assets** under $10,000
- **Assets acquired before 20 September 1985** (pre-CGT assets)
- **Compensation payments** for personal injury

## How a Capital Gain Is Calculated

**Capital gain = Sale price − Cost base**

The **cost base** includes:
- Purchase price
- Stamp duty and legal fees on purchase
- Agent commissions on sale
- Capital improvement costs (major renovations, not maintenance)
- Incidental costs of ownership where they haven't been claimed as deductions

**Example:**
- Bought 500 shares for $20 each = $10,000 cost base (plus $20 brokerage = $10,020)
- Sold for $28 each = $14,000 (less $20 brokerage = $13,980)
- Capital gain = $13,980 − $10,020 = **$3,960**

## The 50% CGT Discount

If you held the asset for **more than 12 months**, individuals and trusts can apply the **50% CGT discount**, halving the taxable gain. Superannuation funds get a one-third (33%) discount; companies get no discount.

Using the example above with the 50% discount:
- Taxable gain = $3,960 × 50% = **$1,980**
- This $1,980 is added to your taxable income for the year

The 12-month holding rule makes timing your sales important. If you're close to the 12-month mark, waiting a few extra weeks can halve your tax bill.

## Capital Losses

If you sell an asset for less than you paid, you make a **capital loss**. Capital losses:

- Can be offset against capital gains in the same year
- Can be carried forward indefinitely to offset future capital gains
- **Cannot** be offset against ordinary income

**Example:** You have a $5,000 capital gain from shares and a $2,000 capital loss from another investment. Net gain = $3,000 (then apply the 50% discount if held 12+ months = $1,500 taxable).

## CGT and Your Tax Return

Capital gains are reported in your tax return under the capital gains schedule. The taxable gain is added to your other income and taxed at your marginal rate. It is not subject to a separate flat rate.

**Example — $90,000 salary + $10,000 capital gain (after discount):**
- Total taxable income = $100,000
- Marginal rate on the $10,000 gain = 30%
- Extra tax = **$3,000**

This is why high earners with large capital gains often look at timing sales to fall in a lower-income year (e.g. after retirement or during parental leave).

## CGT on Your Investment Property

Investment property has some additional CGT rules:

- **Cost base includes** purchase costs, stamp duty, legal fees, and capital improvements (not repairs claimable as deductions)
- **Partial exemption** if the property was your main residence for part of the ownership period
- **Depreciation claw-back** — depreciation claimed as a tax deduction reduces your cost base, increasing the gain on sale
- **Selling costs** (agent fees, legal costs) reduce the sale proceeds

## CGT on Cryptocurrency

The ATO treats cryptocurrency as a capital asset, not currency. Every time you:

- Sell crypto for Australian dollars
- Trade one cryptocurrency for another
- Use crypto to buy goods or services
- Receive crypto as payment (then dispose of it)

...a CGT event occurs. You need records of every transaction including the AUD value at the time. Exchanges often provide transaction histories but the responsibility is yours.

## Minimising CGT Legally

- **Hold for 12+ months** to access the 50% discount
- **Offset gains with losses** — crystallise a loss in the same year as a gain
- **Donate appreciated assets** to a DGR charity (no CGT on the transfer)
- **Time your sales** — consider your income level in the year of sale
- **Contribute the gain to super** — concessional contributions reduce taxable income that would otherwise be stacked with the gain
