The gig economy has made earning extra income easier than ever — but it has also created tax obligations that many people overlook. Whether you drive for a rideshare platform, deliver food, sell handmade goods online, or freelance in your spare time, the ATO expects you to report the income.

## Do You Need to Report Gig Income?

**Yes.** All income earned in Australia — regardless of source or amount — is taxable. There is no de minimis threshold below which gig income can be ignored. Even if you only earned $500 from occasional freelance work, it must be declared.

## Do You Need an ABN?

An **Australian Business Number (ABN)** is required if you operate a business. The ATO has a "are you in business?" test, but as a general rule:

- If you charge clients directly, invoice for services, or run an online store, you need an ABN
- If you work through a platform like Uber, Airtasker, or Fiverr, you should still get one — most platforms require it
- If you're employed by the platform (rare), the employer withholds tax; otherwise you're a contractor

Applying for an ABN is free at abr.gov.au and takes minutes.

## Rideshare and Delivery Drivers: GST From Dollar One

This is the most important unique rule for rideshare and delivery drivers:

**If you provide taxi travel or rideshare services**, you must register for GST **regardless of turnover**. The normal $75,000 threshold does not apply. This means:

- Register for GST immediately when you start driving
- Collect 10% GST on your fares (platforms like Uber typically handle the GST collection and remittance on your behalf, but you must still be registered)
- Lodge quarterly Business Activity Statements (BAS)
- You can claim GST credits on business expenses (fuel, car running costs)

This rule applies to rideshare (Uber, DiDi, Ola) and food delivery (Uber Eats, Menulog, DoorDash) where you are the driver.

## Other Gig Workers: $75,000 GST Threshold

For all other gig work (freelancing, selling goods, Airtasker tasks, tutoring), the standard GST registration threshold applies:

- Under $75,000 annual turnover from all business activities combined: no GST registration required (but you can register voluntarily to claim input credits)
- At or over $75,000: must register and charge GST

## Income Tax on Gig Earnings

All net profit from gig activities is added to your other income (salary, etc.) and taxed at your marginal rate. The tax is calculated on your **net income**, not gross receipts.

**Example:**
- Full-time job salary: $70,000
- Freelance income (gross): $15,000
- Freelance expenses: $3,000 (software, equipment, phone)
- Net freelance income: $12,000
- Total taxable income: $82,000
- The $12,000 is taxed at 30% marginal rate = **$3,600 additional tax**

Because you have no employer withholding tax on the freelance income, you'll likely have a tax bill at year end — or the ATO may put you on PAYG instalments after your first return.

## Deductions for Gig Workers

Running a gig involves real expenses, and these reduce your taxable income:

**Rideshare / delivery drivers:**
- Vehicle running costs (fuel, oil, tyres, insurance, registration, repairs) — the work-related percentage based on a logbook
- Tolls and parking
- Phone mount, dash cam
- Cleaning the car for work purposes

**Freelancers and online workers:**
- Computer, tablet, and phone (work-related portion)
- Software subscriptions (Adobe, design tools, project management)
- Home office costs (67c/hr fixed rate or actual costs)
- Platform fees and payment processing fees
- Professional development and courses
- Business insurance

**Sellers (Etsy, eBay, markets):**
- Cost of goods sold
- Platform fees and shipping costs
- Packaging materials
- Equipment for making products
- Market stall fees

## Separating Business and Personal Finances

Open a separate bank account for your side income. It makes record-keeping dramatically easier and helps you track profitability. The ATO expects clean records — mixing personal and business transactions is a red flag in an audit.

## Super for Gig Workers

You are responsible for your own superannuation. No one else is paying it for you. Many sole traders contribute personally to super and claim the contribution as a tax deduction (up to the $30,000 concessional cap including any employer super from your day job).

## Keeping Records

Keep records for five years:
- All invoices issued and received
- Bank statements for the business account
- Receipts for deductible expenses
- Logbook if claiming vehicle expenses
- Platform transaction histories (download annually)

## Tax Planning for Side Hustles

If your side hustle is growing, consider:

- Whether to structure as a company or trust (relevant once profits are significant)
- Contributing extra to super in high-income years to reduce taxable income
- Timing purchases of depreciable equipment before 30 June
