Every year, Australians leave money on the table by missing legitimate deductions. Others overclaim and face audits or penalties. The goal is to claim everything you're genuinely entitled to — no more, no less. Here's how to do that systematically.

## First: Understand What a Refund Actually Is

A tax refund means you overpaid during the year — your employer withheld more than you actually owe. It's not a bonus from the government; it's your own money coming back.

A large refund means you've been giving the ATO an interest-free loan all year. A better outcome is accurate withholding and a small refund (or nil outcome). That said, if you have deductions to claim, claiming them correctly produces a legitimate refund.

## Strategy 1: Capture Every Work-Related Deduction

Most people claim the obvious things (union fees, professional memberships) and miss the less visible ones. Go through each category:

**Vehicle:** Did you drive between two workplaces, to a client, or to a site other than your normal workplace? Claim it. Use the ATO's cents-per-kilometre method (88 cents/km, up to 5,000 km) or a logbook for higher mileage.

**Home office:** If you worked from home, claim 67 cents per hour for every hour — for the full year, not just a sample period. Keep a log. Also claim any depreciation on work-related equipment.

**Phone and internet:** What percentage of your phone and internet use is work-related? You can claim that proportion. Review one month of calls/data to establish a reasonable percentage.

**Equipment and tools:** Any item costing $300 or less with predominantly work-related use can be claimed in full. Items over $300 are depreciated, but still generate a deduction this year.

**Clothing:** Protective gear, uniforms with an employer logo, occupation-specific clothing. Not regular business attire.

**Self-education:** Courses, books, subscriptions directly related to your current job. Not courses for a new career.

## Strategy 2: Don't Miss Investment Deductions

If you own shares, managed funds, or an investment property, check:

**Shares and funds:**
- Account keeping fees for dedicated investment accounts
- Brokerage fees (add to cost base for CGT, but management fees on managed funds are often deductible)
- Subscription to investment research, financial publications
- Interest on investment loans

**Investment property (see full [negative gearing guide](/blog/negative-gearing-explained/)):**
- Mortgage interest (investment portion only)
- Property management fees
- Repairs and maintenance (not capital improvements)
- Council rates, water, insurance, land tax
- Depreciation (use a quantity surveyor's schedule)
- Travel to inspect (with restrictions — primarily available to investors with multiple properties)

## Strategy 3: Claim Income Protection Insurance

If you pay income protection insurance premiums **outside of super** (i.e. directly to an insurer), the premiums are tax-deductible. This is one of the most commonly missed deductions.

Premiums paid inside super as part of a group policy are generally not individually deductible (the fund claims the deduction), but premiums you pay directly to a retail insurer for income protection are deductible in full.

Life insurance, trauma insurance, and total and permanent disability (TPD) insurance premiums are generally **not** deductible.

## Strategy 4: Claim Last Year's Tax Agent Fee

The fee you paid a tax agent to prepare last year's return is deductible **this year**. If you used a tax agent for the 2024–25 return, the fee appears as a deduction in your 2025–26 return. Don't forget it — it's often $150–$400 that goes unclaimed.

## Strategy 5: Claim All Charitable Donations

Donations of $2 or more to registered **Deductible Gift Recipients (DGRs)** are deductible. Most major charities, community organisations, environmental groups, and arts foundations are DGRs.

Check if the organisation is a DGR at abr.gov.au. Non-DGR donations — religious organisations, community sporting clubs, many school fundraisers — are not deductible even if they feel like charitable giving.

Keep the receipt. Donations under $10 don't require a receipt but you should have a record.

## Strategy 6: Check for Unclaimed Carry-Forward Losses

If you've had capital losses in previous years that weren't fully absorbed by capital gains, they carry forward indefinitely. Review prior tax returns (available in myGov) to see if you have an unabsorbed capital loss balance to claim against this year's gains.

## Strategy 7: Use Pre-Filling but Verify It

myTax pre-fills data from employers, banks, health funds, and super funds. This saves time but isn't always complete:

- Bank interest is often pre-filled but check for accounts the ATO doesn't have data for
- Dividends from small companies or DRP (dividend reinvestment plans) may be missing
- Managed fund distributions can be delayed — you may need to add them manually
- Deductions are never pre-filled — you must enter all of them yourself

## Strategy 8: Consider a Tax Agent for Complex Situations

For a straightforward salary with standard deductions, myTax is sufficient. But if you have:

- Investment properties
- Complex share portfolios with CGT events
- A side business or ABN income
- Multiple income sources
- A HELP debt plus investment income (HELP repayment income rules are complex)

...a registered tax agent often finds deductions that more than cover their fee. Their fee is deductible next year (see Strategy 4).

## What Triggers an ATO Review?

The ATO uses data matching and statistical algorithms to flag unusual returns. Common triggers:

- Deductions significantly higher than similar taxpayers in your occupation
- Work-related expenses that seem inconsistent with your income
- Rental income but no depreciation claimed (flags a missed deduction that could reduce CGT cost base down the line)
- Cryptocurrency not declared while exchange data shows transactions

Claiming legitimate deductions — even large ones — is fine as long as you have records. The ATO auditing is about fraud, not high claims.
