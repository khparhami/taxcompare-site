One of the most consequential financial decisions for Australian workers is whether to operate as an employee or an independent contractor. The tax rules, obligations, and opportunities differ significantly — and getting it wrong can be costly.

## The Legal Distinction Matters First

Before exploring tax, it's important to note that the ATO and Fair Work Australia have their own tests for whether someone is genuinely a contractor or an employee. Sham contracting — where an employer calls someone a contractor to avoid entitlements — carries serious legal penalties.

The key factors the ATO considers:

- **Control:** Can the worker decide how and when the work is done?
- **Results:** Is payment for hours worked (employee) or a specific result (contractor)?
- **Substitution:** Can the worker send someone else to do the job?
- **Tools and equipment:** Who provides them?
- **Risk:** Does the worker bear financial risk for a bad result?

## Tax as an Employee

As an employee, your tax life is relatively straightforward:

- Your employer withholds income tax via **PAYG withholding**
- Your employer pays **superannuation** (12% from 2025–26) on top of your salary
- You have **no GST obligations** on employment income
- Work-related deductions are available but limited (specific to employment)
- At year end, you lodge a return and reconcile

**Take-home pay is predictable.** Your employer handles compliance; your exposure is primarily the annual return.

## Tax as a Contractor

Contractors face a broader set of obligations:

### ABN and GST

If you earn $75,000 or more annually from your contracting activities, you must register for **GST** and charge 10% on top of your invoices. You collect GST from clients, remit it to the ATO quarterly (via a Business Activity Statement), and can claim GST credits on business purchases.

Even below $75,000, it's often worth registering for the GST credits alone.

### Income Tax — No Employer Withholding

Unlike employees, no one withholds tax from contractor invoices (unless you don't provide an ABN, in which case 47% is withheld). You are responsible for setting aside your own tax.

Most contractors put the ATO on **PAYG instalments** — you pay estimated tax quarterly. Failure to set aside enough leads to a large bill plus potential interest charges.

### No Automatic Super

Employers don't pay super on contractor payments in most cases. You need to fund your own retirement by making personal super contributions. You can claim a tax deduction for personal contributions up to the concessional contributions cap ($30,000 in 2025–26), but it's your responsibility to make them.

### Business Deductions

Contractors can claim a much broader range of deductions as legitimate business expenses:

- Home office costs (proportional)
- Equipment, tools, and software
- Professional indemnity insurance
- Accounting and legal fees
- Vehicle expenses (logbook or cents-per-kilometre method)
- Training and professional development
- Mobile phone and internet (business portion)

These deductions can meaningfully reduce your taxable income.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| Item | Employee | Contractor |
|---|---|---|
| Tax withholding | Employer withholds | Must self-manage |
| Superannuation | Employer pays 12% SGC | Must fund yourself |
| GST | Not applicable | Required if turnover ≥$75k |
| BAS lodgement | No | Quarterly |
| Deductions | Limited (work-related) | Broad (business expenses) |
| Leave entitlements | Annual, sick, long service | None |
| Medicare levy | Standard 2% | Standard 2% |
| Tax-free threshold | Applies | Applies |
| PAYG withholding | Via employer | PAYG instalments |

## The Rate Fallacy

A common contractor misconception is that charging a higher day rate than a salaried equivalent automatically means higher take-home pay. The hidden costs:

- **No super** — an employee on $100,000 also receives ~$12,000 in employer super; a contractor must fund this themselves
- **No leave** — 4 weeks annual leave = approximately 8% of income
- **No sick pay** — every sick day is unpaid
- **Higher compliance costs** — accounting, BAS lodgement, insurance

A contractor on $120,000 may take home less than an employee on $100,000 once all costs are accounted for. The breakeven rate depends on your specific deductions and business costs.

## Personal Services Income (PSI)

If most of your contracting income comes from your personal efforts and skills rather than from a business operation, the ATO may classify it as **Personal Services Income (PSI)**. PSI rules limit the deductions you can claim and prevent income-splitting arrangements. The rules are detailed — a tax professional can help you determine whether they apply.
