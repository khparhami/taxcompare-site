Saving for a house deposit is one of the most significant financial challenges for Australians under 40. The First Home Super Saver (FHSS) scheme offers a way to use the tax advantages of superannuation to build a deposit faster than an ordinary savings account.

## What Is the FHSS Scheme?

The FHSS scheme allows eligible first home buyers to make **voluntary contributions** to their superannuation fund and later withdraw those contributions (plus associated earnings) to put toward a first home deposit.

The tax advantage comes from the fact that:

1. Contributions enter super at the **15% contributions tax** rate — lower than most people's marginal income tax rate
2. Withdrawals are taxed at your **marginal rate minus a 30% offset** — making withdrawals cheaper than regular savings for most taxpayers

## How Much Can You Save?

- You can contribute **up to $15,000 per financial year** under the scheme
- The **lifetime cap is $50,000** in total voluntary contributions per person
- Couples can each access the scheme independently, potentially releasing up to **$100,000** combined

## Step-by-Step: How It Works

**Step 1: Make voluntary contributions**

You can make either:
- **Salary sacrifice contributions** (pre-tax): Counts as concessional contributions, taxed at 15% entering the fund
- **After-tax personal contributions**: Non-concessional; you later claim a tax deduction to convert them to concessional

Both types count toward your $15,000 annual / $50,000 lifetime FHSS cap.

All contributions still count toward your normal concessional cap ($30,000) or non-concessional cap ($120,000).

**Step 2: Check your FHSS balance**

Log into myGov → ATO → Superannuation → FHSS. The ATO tracks your eligible contributions and calculates "associated earnings" using the shortfall interest charge rate (a deemed rate, not your actual fund earnings).

**Step 3: Apply for a determination**

Before you sign a purchase contract, request a **FHSS determination** from the ATO via myGov. This tells you the maximum releasable amount. You can make this request before you find a property.

**Step 4: Request a release**

Once you have a determination, request the release. The ATO instructs your super fund to release the funds and withholds tax before paying you. The timing matters — you have 12 months from the release date to sign a contract (or you must recontribute the funds to super or pay a penalty).

**Step 5: Receive the funds and buy your home**

The released amount is deposited into your bank account. Use it for your deposit.

## The Tax Saving in Practice

**Example — $70,000 salary, $15,000 FHSS contribution in 2025–26:**

Without FHSS: Save $15,000 from after-tax take-home pay.
- After income tax at 30% marginal rate, earning $15,000 costs $15,000 ÷ 0.70 = **$21,429 of gross income**.

With FHSS (salary sacrifice): $15,000 enters super, taxed at 15% = $12,750 inside the fund.
- Withdrawal: taxed at marginal rate (30%) minus 30% offset = **0% net tax** in this case.
- You received $12,750 toward your deposit, having only "spent" $15,000 of gross income.

The higher your marginal rate, the larger the advantage.

## Eligibility Requirements

To be eligible you must:

- Be **18 years or older** at the time of withdrawal
- Have **never owned property** in Australia (including investment properties) — this applies independently to each person
- Not have previously applied for an FHSS release
- Intend to genuinely live in the property for at least **6 months** within the first 12 months of ownership
- Be an Australian resident for tax purposes

## What the FHSS Doesn't Cover

- The scheme cannot release **employer SG contributions** or the earnings on those — only voluntary contributions you make yourself
- The scheme does not reduce the purchase price or avoid stamp duty
- Withdrawals do not come with a guarantee — if your fund performed poorly, the deemed earnings rate may be higher than actual growth

## Comparing FHSS to a Regular Savings Account

| Feature | Regular savings account | FHSS scheme |
|---|---|---|
| Tax on contributions | After income tax (marginal rate) | 15% (concessional) |
| Tax on earnings | Marginal rate | Deemed rate, taxed at marginal − 30% |
| Flexibility | Withdraw anytime | Must use for first home |
| Accessibility | Immediate | 12-month process with ATO |
| Amount | Unlimited | Up to $50,000 per person |

For anyone in the 30%+ marginal rate bracket, the FHSS scheme is generally worth using — the tax advantage compounds the longer you contribute. For those in the 16% bracket, the advantage is smaller but still positive.
