Millions of Australians work from home at least part of the time, and a legitimate deduction is available to reduce the cost. The ATO revised the rules in 2022–23 and the revised methods remain in place for 2025–26. Here's everything you need to know.

## The Two Methods

You must use one of two ATO-approved methods. You cannot mix and match within the same income year.

### Method 1: Revised Fixed Rate (67 cents per hour)

The simplest option for most people. You claim **67 cents per hour** for every hour worked at home, and this covers:

- Electricity and gas for heating, cooling, and lighting
- Internet (the work-related portion)
- Mobile and home phone (the work-related portion)
- Computer consumables (printer ink, paper)
- Stationery

You then claim separately for the decline in value (depreciation) of home office equipment and furniture.

**What you need:**
- A record of all hours worked at home for the entire income year — the ATO no longer accepts a four-week representative diary; you must keep a full-year log (a timesheet, roster, or calendar entry is fine)
- Receipts for equipment and furniture claimed separately

**Example:** 900 hours worked from home × $0.67 = **$603 deduction** (before equipment).

### Method 2: Actual Cost Method

More complex but potentially higher if your home office costs are significant. You calculate the actual work-related proportion of each expense:

- **Occupancy costs** (rent, mortgage interest, rates, home insurance) — only claimable if your home is a "place of business" (rare for employees; more relevant to sole traders)
- **Running costs** — electricity, internet, phone, cleaning proportional to home office use
- **Equipment depreciation** — full work-related portion

**What you need:**
- Receipts for all expenses
- Floor area calculations (home office m² ÷ total home m²) for occupancy costs
- Detailed usage logs for phone and internet

Most employees should not claim occupancy costs — doing so may trigger a capital gains tax issue when you sell the home if part of it is deemed a "place of business."

## What You Can Also Claim (Either Method)

Regardless of which method you use, you can separately claim:

- **Decline in value** of depreciable assets: work-related portion of laptop, monitor, desk, chair, headset, webcam, printer, etc.
- **Repair costs** for home office equipment
- **Work-related phone calls** (if not covered by the fixed rate)

For equipment costing **$300 or less** with a work-related use above 50%, you can claim the full cost immediately rather than depreciating it.

## What You Cannot Claim

- Coffee, tea, snacks — personal expenses
- Rent or mortgage interest (for employees without a genuine business-use room)
- General household items (furniture used for personal purposes, main TV)
- Anything reimbursed by your employer

## Keeping Records

The ATO has been increasingly focused on WFH claims. Keep:

- A full-year log of work-from-home hours (digital calendar, timesheet, or diary entries)
- Receipts for equipment and consumables
- Internet and phone bills if claiming actual costs
- Your employer's written confirmation that you worked from home (or a working-from-home arrangement letter)

Records must be kept for **five years** from the date of lodgement.

## The Employer-Provided Equipment Exception

If your employer provides you with a laptop, phone, or other equipment, you **cannot claim depreciation** on that item — you don't own it. You may still be able to claim the work-related portion of your own equipment if you use it alongside employer-provided items.

## What's the Actual Saving?

If you're on a $90,000 salary (marginal rate 30%) and claim $800 in WFH deductions:

- Tax saving = $800 × 30% = **$240**
