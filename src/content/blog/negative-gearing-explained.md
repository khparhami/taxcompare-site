Negative gearing is one of Australia's most discussed — and most misunderstood — tax strategies. It's used by hundreds of thousands of property investors, yet many people entering the property market aren't entirely sure what it means or whether it's actually beneficial. Here's a clear explanation.

## What Is Negative Gearing?

An investment property is **negatively geared** when the income it generates (rent) is less than the costs of owning it (mortgage interest, council rates, repairs, depreciation, and other expenses). The net loss can then be deducted against your other income — typically your salary — reducing your taxable income and tax bill.

The term "gearing" simply means borrowing money to invest. If your investment makes more than it costs, it's positively geared. If it costs more than it earns, it's negatively geared.

## A Simple Example

**Investor: $120,000 salary, investment property in 2025–26**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Rental income | $24,000 |
| Mortgage interest | $28,000 |
| Council rates & water | $2,500 |
| Property management fees | $2,400 |
| Repairs and maintenance | $1,500 |
| Depreciation (building & fittings) | $3,600 |
| **Net rental loss** | **−$14,000** |

Without the property, taxable income = $120,000 → tax ≈ $26,788 (2025–26, before Medicare).

With the property, taxable income = $120,000 − $14,000 = $106,000 → tax ≈ $21,988.

**Tax saving = $4,800** (at 37% marginal rate on a $13,000 taxable loss after LITO phase-out).

## What Can You Deduct?

As a landlord, deductible expenses include:

**Immediately deductible (in the year incurred):**
- Mortgage interest (investment loan only — not principal repayments)
- Property management and agent fees
- Council rates and water rates
- Land tax
- Building and contents insurance
- Repairs and maintenance (fixing existing damage — not improvements)
- Advertising for tenants
- Pest control, cleaning, gardening
- Stationery, postage, phone calls related to the property
- Travel to inspect the property (with significant restrictions since 2017)

**Deductible over time (depreciation):**
- **Division 43 (building allowance):** 2.5% per year of the original construction cost for buildings constructed after 18 July 1985
- **Division 40 (plant and equipment):** Depreciating assets like appliances, carpet, blinds — based on effective life. Note: for properties purchased after 9 May 2017, second-hand plant and equipment items cannot be depreciated by the new owner.

A quantity surveyor can prepare a **depreciation schedule** that maximises your claims and is typically tax-deductible itself.

## What You Cannot Deduct

- Principal repayments on your mortgage (only interest is deductible)
- Personal-use portion if you or family use the property
- Initial costs of acquiring the property (stamp duty, legal fees) — these go into your cost base for CGT purposes
- Capital improvements (these add to cost base, not immediate deductions)
- Expenses while the property is not genuinely available for rent

## Positive Gearing

If your rental income exceeds your expenses, you have a **positively geared** or **positively cashflowed** property. The net rental income is added to your taxable income and taxed at your marginal rate. Positive gearing is generally better for cash flow but results in a higher tax bill.

## The CGT Interaction

The tax benefit of negative gearing is partially offset at sale. Because the property was used to produce income:

- It is subject to CGT when you sell
- Depreciation claimed reduces your cost base (increasing the CGT gain)
- The 50% CGT discount applies if held 12+ months

A property with large depreciation deductions taken over 10 years will have a lower cost base and a larger CGT bill on sale. The strategy works best when the tax deductions now are worth more (high marginal rate during working years) than the CGT on sale (potentially in retirement at a lower marginal rate).

## Does Negative Gearing Actually Make Sense?

The tax benefit is real but should not be the primary reason to buy an investment property. The benefit depends on:

- **Your marginal rate** — at 30% you save 30c per dollar of loss; at 45% you save 45c
- **Capital growth** — the property needs to grow in value to eventually profit; you're accepting a cash-flow loss each year in anticipation of this
- **Vacancy and unexpected costs** — a vacant property generates no rent but all the costs remain

A property generating an annual loss of $10,000 while owned for 10 years and then sold creates $100,000 of accumulated losses offset against income — but also likely a substantial CGT liability on sale. Run the full numbers, not just the annual tax saving.
